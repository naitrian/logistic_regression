How it works

The prediction pipeline

1

You enter your health data

15 clinical features—demographics, smoking habits, medical history, and vitals—collected the same way the original Framingham study recorded them.

2

Values are standardized

Each continuous variable is scaled using the training set's mean and standard deviation, so the model sees data in the same distribution it learned from.

3

Logistic regression computes your risk

A weighted sum of your features is passed through the sigmoid function, producing a probability between 0 and 1. If that probability exceeds the optimized threshold of 0.152, the prediction flips to "at risk."

4

You get a clear result

The raw probability and the binary prediction are shown, along with an explanation of what the numbers mean for you.

About the model

Performance & limitations

Accuracy

67.57%

Out of every 100 people this model evaluates, it gives the correct yes/no answer about 68 times. Think of it like a weather forecast that's right roughly 7 out of 10 days—not perfect, but meaningfully better than guessing.

Recall

67.67%

Of all the people who will actually develop heart disease, this model correctly flags about 68% of them. The remaining ~32% get a false "you're fine" — which is why this tool is a conversation starter, not a diagnosis.

Important disclaimer

This is an educational demonstration built on publicly available Framingham Heart Study data. It is not a medical device and should never replace professional medical advice. If you have concerns about your heart health, consult a cardiologist.

Dataset: Framingham Heart Study — a long-term cardiovascular cohort study that began in 1948 in Framingham, Massachusetts.

Algorithm: Logistic regression with gradient descent (10,000 iterations, learning rate 1.0). No external ML libraries — trained from scratch with NumPy.

Train/test split: 80/20, randomly shuffled. Missing values filled with training medians. Continuous features standardized using training-set statistics.

Decision threshold: 0.152 (optimized for recall rather than default 0.5, since missing a true positive is far more dangerous than a false alarm in medical contexts).
